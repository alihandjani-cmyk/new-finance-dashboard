ℹ️ Ranking Methodology

The composite rank is the simple average of up to four independent liquidity sub-ranks for each exchange, equal ¼ weight each. Lower average rank = more liquid = closer to #1.

📊 Volume rank — exchanges ranked by most recent daily turnover (highest = rank 1). LSE and Euronext use official exchange publications; Nasdaq 100, NYSE, Xetra, and SIX use universe-based yfinance estimates. Volume uses the latest available value per exchange rather than a date-matched lookup, since official files arrive next morning and date alignment would otherwise distort the composite.

💧 Amihud ILLIQ rank — exchanges ranked by market-wide average Amihud (2002) illiquidity ratio (lowest = rank 1). Computed from 20-day daily OHLCV per stock, equal-weighted across all constituents. This is the richest time-series signal and drives most of the 10-day heatmap.

🔄 Turnover Ratio rank — exchanges ranked by daily trading volume as a percentage of total market capitalisation (highest = rank 1). A market where a higher fraction of its capitalisation trades each day is considered more liquid. Computed as aggregate daily turnover (£/€/) divided by the sum of market caps across index constituents.

↗️ Abdi-Ranaldo Spread rank — exchanges ranked by Abdi-Ranaldo (2017) range-based spread using daily High, Low and Close (lowest = rank 1). Outperforms Roll for liquid large-cap stocks. Requires minimum 12 trading days of valid OHLCV data.

Data completeness policy: The ranking and heatmap only include trading days where all nine exchanges have complete Amihud ILLIQ data. Today is excluded from rankings while markets are open (before 20:00 UTC); after NYSE close all end-of-day data is included. LSE and Euronext publish official volume statistics the following morning; US markets close at ~21:00 UTC. Cells marked — indicate a metric was unavailable for that exchange on that date (e.g. a market holiday, or spread history still accumulating in early runs).

💧 Amihud Illiquidity (ILLIQ)
ILLIQ = |Return| / Dollar Volume (per $1M traded, FX-normalized to USD so all exchanges are directly comparable regardless of currency). Lower = more liquid. Computed from 20-day OHLCV data via Yahoo Finance.

📖 Methodology

Amihud (2002) Illiquidity Ratio (ILLIQ) measures price impact per unit of trading volume — the classic market microstructure proxy for illiquidity.

Formula: ILLIQi,t = |Ri,t (%)| / DVOLi,t (USD millions)

Currency normalisation: LSE prices are quoted in pence, so local DVOL = Close(p) × Volume / 100 / 1,000,000 (£M); all other exchanges: DVOL = Close × Volume / 1,000,000 (local currency M). Euronext .OL (Oslo) tickers are converted NOK→EUR using the live NOKEUR=X rate. DVOL is then converted to USD millions using each run's live FX rates before computing ILLIQ — this keeps the ratio comparable across exchanges regardless of currency (1M KRW and 1M GBP aren't the same amount of money, so ranking on raw local-currency DVOL would unfairly favor exchanges denominated in low-value currencies).

Rolling window: Each day's ILLIQ is the equal-weighted average across all index constituents with sufficient data. The market average is the mean of each stock's 20-day average ILLIQ.

Interpretation: Higher ILLIQ = less liquid (larger price move per $1M USD-equivalent traded). Rank 1 in the Liquidity Ranking tab = lowest ILLIQ = most liquid.

📚 Academic Reference

Amihud, Y. (2002). "Illiquidity and stock returns: Cross-section and time-series effects." Journal of Financial Markets, 5(1), 31–56.
doi:10.1016/S1386-4181(01)00024-6

📖 Methodology

Abdi & Ranaldo (2017) Range-Based Spread improves on Roll by incorporating intraday price ranges (daily High and Low), making it more robust for liquid large-cap stocks where the bid-ask bounce in close prices is very small.

Formula: ct = ln(Ct) − ½(ln(Ht) + ln(Lt)), then S% = 2 × √(−Cov(ct, ct−1)) × 100.

Intuition: ct captures the position of the close price within the day's trading range — a proxy for whether the day ended at the bid or the ask. Negative serial covariance in ct reflects the bid-ask bounce, amplified by the full intraday range.

Advantage over Roll: Uses more price information (H, L, C) rather than close prices alone. Empirically outperforms Roll for highly liquid stocks and produces fewer missing estimates on trending days.

Data: 30 trading days of daily OHLCV data via Yahoo Finance. Market average = equal-weighted mean across constituents with valid estimates (minimum 12 days required).

📚 Academic Reference

Abdi, F. & Ranaldo, A. (2017). "A Simple Estimation of Bid-Ask Spreads from Daily Close, High, and Low Prices." The Review of Financial Studies, 30(12), 4437–4480.
doi:10.1093/rfs/hhx084

🔄 Daily Turnover Ratio
Turnover Ratio = Daily Trading Volume (currency B) ÷ Total Market Cap (currency B) × 100%. Higher ratio = more actively traded relative to market size = more liquid.

📖 Methodology

Turnover Ratio normalises trading activity by market size. Raw daily volume (in £/€/$ billions) reflects absolute size — a large exchange naturally has more volume. Dividing by total market capitalisation shows how actively traded the market is relative to its size: a ratio of 0.1% means 0.1% of the entire market changed hands that day.

Formula: TR% = (Σ Closei × Volumei) / (Σ Market Capi) × 100, where the sum is over all index constituents tracked by the dashboard.

Why it matters: Two exchanges with identical raw volume can have very different liquidity profiles if one is much larger than the other. Turnover ratio corrects for this, giving a size-neutral view of market activity. Academic literature (Datar, Naik & Radcliffe 1998) shows stocks with higher turnover ratios command lower liquidity premiums.

Data: Volume is computed from yfinance OHLCV data for each exchange's index constituents. Market cap is the sum of (price × shares outstanding) across the same universe. Both refreshed on each full workflow run (4× daily). Currency is native to each exchange (GBP for LSE, EUR for Euronext/Xetra, USD for Nasdaq/NYSE, CHF for SIX).

Role in ranking: Turnover Ratio replaces Roll (1984) spread as the third of four composite signals, alongside Amihud ILLIQ, Abdi-Ranaldo (2017) AR spread, and raw Volume — giving the composite four conceptually distinct dimensions: price impact, transaction cost, relative activity, and absolute activity.

📚 Academic Reference

Datar, V. T., Naik, N. Y., & Radcliffe, R. (1998). "Liquidity and stock returns: An alternative test." Journal of Financial Markets, 1(2), 203–219.

Amihud, Y. & Mendelson, H. (1986). "Asset pricing and the bid-ask spread." Journal of Financial Economics, 17(2), 223–249.

🏦 About Market Capitalisation Data

Market capitalisation = share price × shares outstanding, denominated in the exchange's home currency. Rankings show the top 10 constituents by market cap within each index universe as of the most recent trading session.

Data source: Yahoo Finance OHLCV data via yfinance. Prices are as of the previous close (intraday prices are not used). Shares outstanding are sourced from Yahoo Finance's fast_info API.

Currency note: LSE prices are quoted in pence (GBX); market cap is displayed in GBP billions. All other exchanges display in their native currency (EUR for Euronext and Xetra, USD for Nasdaq/NYSE, CHF for SIX Swiss).

Update frequency: Refreshed up to 4× daily. Rankings may shift intraday as prices move but shares outstanding data updates less frequently.

📈 About Top 10 Movers

Shows the top 10 stocks by percentage change compared to the previous trading day's close, within each exchange's index universe. Positive moves are shown in green, negative moves in red. On very quiet days some slots may be filled by small decliners.

Calculation: % Gain = (Today's Close − Previous Close) / Previous Close × 100. Requires at least 2 trading days of data to compute a valid return.

LSE note: Prices are quoted in pence (p). The "Price" column shows pence, and the "Change" column shows the pence move.

Data source: Yahoo Finance daily OHLCV (2-day window). Updated up to 4× daily — same-day prices are available from market open in the relevant timezone.
